                    I. FRAMEWORK OVERVIEW & CORE FORMULA
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

WHAT IS OMEGA (Ω)?

Omega (Ω) is a thermodynamically-grounded metric for assessing the viability of 
complex adaptive systems. It quantifies whether a system (couple, organization, 
individual consciousness) can maintain coherent function over time or is likely 
to collapse, disintegrate, or experience crisis.

THE CORE FORMULA (Standard Scale, N ≥ 50):

    Ω = [Positive / (Entropy × 1.0 + Abuse × 0.5)] - ConcentrationPenalty ± TemporalAdjustments

Where:
    Positive = (IAE + CA + HA + HE + dX/d + RA) / 6
    Entropy = (Smicro + Smeso + Smacro + Sthermo + Spsy + Stemporal) / 6
    Abuse = (ADR + EFI + HERS + CI + Severity/Prevalence) / 5 × (1 - CI)
    
    ConcentrationPenalty = 1.0 if any single construct > 8.0; else 0
    
    TemporalAdjustments:
        - If dΠ/dt < -0.5/year: -1.5 penalty (rapid coherence decline)
        - If Tstab < 0.3: -1.0 × (0.3-Tstab)/0.3 penalty (high instability)
        - If Π_leader < 5.0 AND ρ > 0.6: -0.1 × (5.0-Π_leader) × ρ (leader contagion)

INTERPRETATION THRESHOLDS:

    Ω > 6.5  →  Thriving system (sustained growth likely)
    5.0-6.5  →  Viable system (normal risks managed)
    3.5-5.0  →  Stressed system (intervention window open)
    2.5-3.5  →  Crisis system (survival uncertain)
    Ω < 2.5  →  Collapse imminent (emergency intervention required)

MICRO-SCALE ADAPTATION (N = 2-10, e.g., couples):

    Ω_micro = [Positive / (Entropy × 1.0 + Abuse × 0.5)]
    
    No concentration penalty (too few elements for meaningful concentration)
    Confidence bands: ± (3.0 - 1.0 × ln(N))
        N=2:  ±1.67
        N=5:  ±1.06
        N=10: ±0.75

CONSCIOUSNESS ADAPTATION (N = 1, single consciousness):

    Ω_consciousness = [IAE + CA + HA + HE + dX/d + RA] / 6
                    / (Spsy_proxy × 1.0 + Entropy_neural × 0.5)
    
    Where Spsy_proxy = average of (behavioral + linguistic + physiological proxies)
    Confidence band: ±2.0 (proxies less reliable than direct measurement)

THEORETICAL FOUNDATION:

The framework draws from:
- Thermodynamic entropy (Second Law: systems tend toward disorder without energy input)
- Information theory (Shannon entropy: uncertainty in system state)
- Complex adaptive systems theory (emergence, phase transitions, critical points)
- Relational dynamics (attachment theory, coordination theory, Gottman research)
- Neuroscience (consciousness as information integration, predictive processing)

KEY PRINCIPLE: All systems require continuous energy investment to maintain low 
entropy (high predictability, low chaos). Without this investment, entropy rises, 
uncertainty increases, and collapse becomes more likely.

              II. CATEGORY 1: POSITIVE FACTORS (7 CONSTRUCTS)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

These constructs represent protective factors, adaptive capacities, and resources 
that increase system viability. Higher scores on positive factors indicate greater 
resilience, flexibility, and sustainability.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #1: IAE (INFORMATION ADAPTATION EFFICIENCY)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Information Adaptation Efficiency
ABBREVIATION: IAE
SCALE: 0-10 (continuous)
CATEGORY: Positive Factor

DEFINITION:
The system's capacity to process, integrate, and respond adaptively to new 
information from the environment or internal states. High IAE indicates the 
system can learn, update beliefs, and adjust behavior based on feedback.

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Information theory: Systems that process information efficiently reduce 
  uncertainty and increase predictability
- Cybernetics: Adaptive feedback loops enable homeostasis and goal pursuit
- Cognitive psychology: Cognitive flexibility and openness to experience

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 2 minutes):
        Method: LIWC (Linguistic Inquiry & Word Count) complexity scan
        - Analyze communication sample for linguistic complexity
        - Count: abstract vs. concrete language ratio, vocabulary diversity
        - Score: 10 - (rigidity_score/max_rigidity) × 10
        Confidence: ±0.3

    Tier 2 (Standard, 15 minutes):
        Method: Rotter Locus of Control Scale + structured interview
        - Administer Rotter LOC (measures perceived control over outcomes)
        - Interview: "Describe a recent surprise. How did you respond?"
        - Rate: Information integration (Did they update beliefs?) 1-10
        - Rate: Behavioral flexibility (Did actions change?) 1-10
        - Score: Average of LOC + interview ratings
        Confidence: ±0.15

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, 3 hours):
        Method: 90-minute narrative interview + multi-rater coding
        - Life narrative: "Tell your story, including major changes"
        - Code for: belief revision frequency, integration of contradictions,
          behavioral adaptation examples, abstract thinking capacity
        - Multi-rater coding (ICC ≥ 0.75 required)
        - Longitudinal tracking (3 assessments over 90 days)
        Confidence: ±0.08

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Communication quality, emotional expression clarity
        - High IAE (8-10): Partners understand each other's needs; adapt to feedback
        - Low IAE (0-4): Miscommunication frequent; rigid response patterns

    Organizations: Strategic flexibility, market responsiveness
        - High IAE (8-10): Org pivots effectively; learns from failures
        - Low IAE (0-4): Org ignores market signals; resists change

    Consciousness: Cognitive flexibility, belief updating
        - High IAE (8-10): Person integrates new experiences; revises worldview
        - Low IAE (0-4): Cognitive rigidity; dogmatic thinking

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: 7-10
    Viable systems: 5-7
    Stressed systems: 3-5
    Crisis systems: 0-3

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Inversely correlated with Spsy (psychological entropy): High IAE reduces 
      internal chaos by integrating contradictions
    - Moderates relationship between HA (heterogeneity) and coordination: High 
      IAE allows diverse systems to coordinate effectively
    - Amplifies effects of HE (environmental enrichment): Systems with high IAE 
      benefit more from novel experiences


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #2: CA_gen (GENERATIVE COORDINATION ALLOCATION)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Generative Coordination Allocation
ABBREVIATION: CA_gen
SCALE: 0-10 (continuous)
CATEGORY: Positive Factor

DEFINITION:
The system's capacity to create new patterns of coordination that didn't exist 
before. Generative coordination produces emergent solutions, novel strategies, 
and innovative problem-solving. This is the "creation" mode of coordination.

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Complex systems theory: Emergence of higher-order patterns from local interactions
- Organizational behavior: Generative vs. adaptive organizational capacity
- Relational dynamics: Co-creation in partnerships; joint problem construction

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 5 minutes):
        Method: Churn rate count (how often system changes approach)
        - Ask: "In the last month, how many times did you try a completely new 
          approach to a recurring problem?"
        - Count: Novel solutions attempted (not repetition of old strategies)
        - Score: Min(attempts/month, 10)
        Confidence: ±0.25

    Tier 2 (Standard, 6 hours over 1 week):
        Method: Weekly interaction diary + trained coder analysis
        - System members complete daily diary: "Today's problem-solving"
        - Code each entry for: generative (new solution) vs. routine (old solution)
        - Calculate: % generative interactions
        - Score: (% generative / 10) × 10
        Confidence: ±0.12

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, 12 weeks):
        Method: Longitudinal diary + inter-rater reliability (ICC ≥ 0.70)
        - 12-week daily diary by all system members
        - Two independent raters code all entries
        - Statistical analysis: generativity trajectory over time
        - Compare to outcome: Did generative coordination predict success?
        Confidence: ±0.07

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Co-creating solutions together (not negotiating pre-set positions)
        - High CA_gen (8-10): Partners brainstorm novel compromises; invent new rituals
        - Low CA_gen (0-4): Partners repeat failed strategies; rigid negotiation

    Organizations: Innovation capacity, strategic pivots
        - High CA_gen (8-10): Org generates new products/processes; adapts to disruption
        - Low CA_gen (0-4): Org relies on legacy strategies; resists innovation

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: 6-10 (high innovation)
    Viable systems: 4-6 (moderate innovation)
    Stressed systems: 2-4 (low innovation, firefighting)
    Crisis systems: 0-2 (no generativity, pure survival mode)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Complementary to CA_comp (compensatory coordination): High CA_gen + high 
      CA_comp = robust coordination (create new solutions AND stabilize existing ones)
    - Enhanced by HA (heterogeneity): Diverse systems generate more novel solutions
    - Reduced by high Entropy: Chaotic systems cannot sustain generative processes


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #3: CA_comp (COMPENSATORY COORDINATION ALLOCATION)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Compensatory Coordination Allocation
ABBREVIATION: CA_comp
SCALE: 0-10 (continuous)
CATEGORY: Positive Factor

DEFINITION:
The system's capacity to maintain existing coordination patterns under stress 
by compensating for disruptions. When one element fails, others step in to 
maintain function. This is the "stabilization" mode of coordination.

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Resilience theory: Redundancy and compensatory mechanisms enable recovery
- Organizational behavior: Cross-training, backup systems, flexible roles
- Relational dynamics: Partner compensation in couples (when one struggles, 
  the other compensates)

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 5 minutes):
        Method: Crisis response count
        - Ask: "In the last month, when something went wrong, how often did 
          someone/something step in to prevent total breakdown?"
        - Count: Successful compensations (crisis averted)
        - Score: Min(compensations/month, 10)
        Confidence: ±0.25

    Tier 2 (Standard, 6 hours over 1 week):
        Method: Weekly interaction diary + compensation coding
        - System members complete daily diary: "Today's challenges & responses"
        - Code each entry for: compensation events (someone stepped in)
        - Calculate: % challenges successfully compensated
        - Score: (% compensated / 10) × 10
        Confidence: ±0.12

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, 12 weeks):
        Method: Longitudinal diary + stress-test experiments
        - 12-week daily diary tracking compensations
        - Introduce controlled stressors (with consent); measure response
        - Multi-rater coding of compensation effectiveness
        - Predict: Does CA_comp at baseline predict resilience at follow-up?
        Confidence: ±0.07

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Partner stepping up when the other is struggling
        - High CA_comp (8-10): When Partner A is sick, B handles everything seamlessly
        - Low CA_comp (0-4): When Partner A struggles, household collapses

    Organizations: Backup systems, cross-functional support
        - High CA_comp (8-10): When team member absent, others cover without crisis
        - Low CA_comp (0-4): Single-point failures cascade into system breakdowns

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: 7-10 (robust compensation)
    Viable systems: 5-7 (moderate compensation)
    Stressed systems: 3-5 (compensation under strain)
    Crisis systems: 0-3 (no compensation capacity; failures cascade)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Complementary to CA_gen: CA_comp stabilizes; CA_gen innovates
    - Enhanced by RA (recovery potential): Systems that can recover quickly 
      have more resources available for compensation
    - Depleted by high Sthermo (thermodynamic entropy): Exhausted systems 
      cannot compensate effectively


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #4: HA (HETEROGENEITY ALLOCATION)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Heterogeneity Allocation
ABBREVIATION: HA
SCALE: 0-10 (continuous)
CATEGORY: Positive Factor

DEFINITION:
The diversity of roles, skills, perspectives, and resources within the system. 
Heterogeneous systems have more adaptive capacity because different elements 
can respond to different challenges. Homogeneous systems are brittle.

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Biodiversity theory: Diverse ecosystems are more resilient to shocks
- Organizational diversity: Cognitive diversity improves problem-solving
- Complexity science: Heterogeneity enables adaptive capacity

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 10 minutes):
        Method: Quick role inventory
        - List all roles/functions in system (e.g., income-earner, caregiver, 
          decision-maker, emotional support)
        - Count: How many different roles? How evenly distributed?
        - Score: (# unique roles / max expected) × 10
        Confidence: ±0.3

    Tier 2 (Standard, 2 hours):
        Method: Diversity audit + Perceived Diversity Scale (PDS)
        - Interview all members: "What unique skills/perspectives do you bring?"
        - Administer PDS questionnaire (validated scale for diversity perception)
        - Quantify: Role diversity, skill diversity, perspective diversity
        - Score: Composite of three dimensions (each 0-10)
        Confidence: ±0.15

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, 12 months):
        Method: Longitudinal diversity tracking + outcome correlation
        - Baseline diversity assessment
        - Track over 12 months: Does diversity change? How?
        - Outcome: Does baseline diversity predict resilience to unexpected shocks?
        - Multi-method triangulation (self-report + observer ratings + network analysis)
        Confidence: ±0.08

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Role specialization vs. role flexibility
        - High HA (8-10): Partners have distinct but complementary skills; can swap roles
        - Low HA (0-4): Partners rigidly locked into identical or single roles

    Organizations: Cognitive diversity, skill variety, demographic diversity
        - High HA (8-10): Teams include diverse expertise, backgrounds, perspectives
        - Low HA (0-4): Homogeneous teams (groupthink risk, single-mode failure)

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: 6-10 (high diversity enables adaptation)
    Viable systems: 4-6 (moderate diversity)
    Stressed systems: 2-4 (low diversity limits response options)
    Crisis systems: 0-2 (extreme homogeneity; single-point failure risk)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Amplifies CA_gen (generative coordination): Diverse systems create more 
      novel solutions
    - Requires high IAE to coordinate: Heterogeneity without information 
      processing becomes chaos
    - Can increase Smeso (mesoscale entropy) if coordination fails: Diversity 
      without integration = fragmentation


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #5: HE (ENVIRONMENTAL ENRICHMENT)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Environmental Enrichment
ABBREVIATION: HE
SCALE: 0-10 (continuous)
CATEGORY: Positive Factor

DEFINITION:
The degree to which the system is exposed to novel, stimulating, and growth-
promoting experiences. Enriched environments provide learning opportunities, 
prevent stagnation, and maintain engagement. Impoverished environments lead 
to atrophy.

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Neuroscience: Environmental enrichment promotes neuroplasticity and resilience
- Ecological psychology: Complexity of environment shapes adaptive capacity
- Relationship maintenance: Novel experiences strengthen bonds (Aron et al.)

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 5 minutes):
        Method: Stimulus count
        - Ask: "In the last month, how many new activities/experiences/
          conversations did you have that were meaningfully different from routine?"
        - Count: Novel experiences (not repetitions)
        - Score: Min(novel_experiences/month, 10)
        Confidence: ±0.25

    Tier 2 (Standard, 4 hours):
        Method: Activity log audit
        - System members maintain 1-week activity log (daily entries)
        - Code each activity: Novel vs. Routine, Engaging vs. Passive
        - Calculate: % time spent in novel + engaging activities
        - Score: (% enriching_time / 10) × 10
        Confidence: ±0.12

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, 90 days):
        Method: Longitudinal activity tracking + outcome correlation
        - 90-day activity log with detailed coding
        - Measure engagement quality (self-report + physiological markers)
        - Outcome: Does enrichment predict vitality/engagement at follow-up?
        - Test intervention: Increase enrichment; measure effect on Ω
        Confidence: ±0.08

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Shared novel experiences, adventures, learning together
        - High HE (8-10): Regular date nights, new activities, intellectual conversations
        - Low HE (0-4): Repetitive routine, no novelty, boredom

    Organizations: Learning culture, innovation opportunities, autonomy
        - High HE (8-10): Employees have learning budgets, cross-functional projects
        - Low HE (0-4): Repetitive tasks, no development, stagnant environment

    Consciousness: Mental stimulation, cognitive engagement, learning
        - High HE (8-10): Person reads, explores ideas, engages in complex thought
        - Low HE (0-4): Passive consumption, no intellectual challenge, mental atrophy

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: 6-10 (continuous growth and exploration)
    Viable systems: 4-6 (moderate novelty)
    Stressed systems: 2-4 (novelty replaced by firefighting)
    Crisis systems: 0-2 (complete stagnation; survival mode)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Requires IAE to integrate: Enrichment without adaptive capacity = overwhelm
    - Buffers against Spsy (psychological entropy): Novel experiences reduce 
      cognitive stagnation
    - Enhanced by high Tstab (temporal stability): Stable systems can explore; 
      chaotic systems cannot


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #6: dX/d (TRAJECTORY)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Trajectory (Rate of Change in System State)
ABBREVIATION: dX/d or dXd
SCALE: 0-10 (with negative values possible for decline)
CATEGORY: Positive Factor (when positive)

DEFINITION:
The direction and speed of change in overall system quality over time. Positive 
trajectory indicates improvement, growth, or recovery. Negative trajectory 
indicates decline, deterioration, or approaching collapse. Zero trajectory 
indicates stagnation.

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Dynamical systems theory: Phase space trajectories predict future states
- Outcome prediction research: Slope of change predicts endpoint
- Clinical psychology: Therapy trajectory predicts final outcome (early rapid 
  response predicts success)

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 5 minutes):
        Method: Recent slope visual inspection
        - Ask: "Over the past 3 months, has your situation gotten better, 
          stayed the same, or gotten worse?"
        - Visual rating scale: -10 (much worse) to 0 (same) to +10 (much better)
        - Score: Rating value (convert to 0-10 scale by adding 10, dividing by 2)
        Confidence: ±0.3

    Tier 2 (Standard, 4 hours):
        Method: Monthly tracking with quantitative metrics
        - Collect 3-6 monthly snapshots of key system metric (satisfaction, 
          performance, health indicator)
        - Calculate linear regression slope: Δmetric / Δtime
        - Normalize slope to 0-10 scale (negative slopes = 0-5, positive = 5-10)
        - Score: Normalized slope
        Confidence: ±0.15

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, 12 months):
        Method: Quarterly assessment + growth curve modeling
        - Collect 4-12 quarterly assessments of comprehensive system state
        - Fit growth curve model (linear, quadratic, or exponential)
        - Extract slope parameter and confidence interval
        - Validate: Does early trajectory predict long-term outcome?
        Confidence: ±0.08

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Relationship quality trajectory
        - Positive dX/d (6-10): Satisfaction increasing, conflicts decreasing
        - Flat dX/d (4-6): Stable (could be stable-good or stable-poor)
        - Negative dX/d (0-4): Satisfaction declining, conflicts increasing

    Organizations: Performance trajectory
        - Positive dX/d (6-10): Revenue/productivity/engagement improving
        - Negative dX/d (0-4): Performance declining, crisis approaching

    Therapy: Symptom change trajectory
        - Positive dX/d (6-10): Symptoms decreasing, functioning improving
        - Negative dX/d (0-4): Symptoms worsening, therapy not working

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: 7-10 (strong positive trajectory)
    Viable systems: 5-7 (slight improvement or stable)
    Stressed systems: 3-5 (flat or slight decline)
    Crisis systems: 0-3 (rapid decline)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Moderated by Tstab (temporal stability): High Tstab + positive dX/d = 
      sustainable growth. Low Tstab + positive dX/d = unstable improvement.
    - Influences dΠ/dt (coherence velocity): Positive trajectory often 
      accompanies increasing coherence
    - Can be adjusted by Tstab factor in formula: If Tstab low, reduce weight 
      of positive dX/d (unstable improvement doesn't count as much)


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #7: RA (RECOVERY ALLOCATION / RECOVERY POTENTIAL)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Recovery Allocation (Recovery Potential)
ABBREVIATION: RA
SCALE: 0-10 (continuous)
CATEGORY: Positive Factor

DEFINITION:
The system's capacity to bounce back from setbacks, repair damage, and restore 
function after stress or crisis. High RA indicates resilience: the system can 
absorb shocks and recover quickly. Low RA indicates brittleness: small shocks 
cause cascading failures.

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Resilience theory: Capacity to absorb disturbance and reorganize
- Gottman research: Repair attempts in relationships predict longevity
- Organizational resilience: Backup resources, redundancy, slack
- Neuroscience: Stress recovery capacity (HRV, cortisol regulation)

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 5 minutes):
        Method: Clinical judgment or self-report
        - Ask: "After a conflict/setback, how quickly can you get back to normal?"
        - Rating scale: 0 (never recover) to 10 (recover immediately)
        - Score: Self-reported rating
        Confidence: ±0.3

    Tier 2 (Standard, 20 minutes):
        Method: Validated outcome + resilience scale
        - Administer Brief Resilience Scale (BRS) or Connor-Davidson Resilience 
          Scale (CD-RISC)
        - Interview: "Describe your worst recent conflict. What happened after?"
        - Code: Time to recovery, quality of repair, resources mobilized
        - Score: Composite of scale + interview (each weighted 50%)
        Confidence: ±0.12

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, 12 months):
        Method: Longitudinal tracking + stress test + outcome verification
        - Baseline RA assessment
        - Introduce controlled stressor (with consent) or wait for natural stressor
        - Measure: Time to recovery, quality of recovery (better/same/worse than before)
        - 12-month follow-up: Did baseline RA predict actual recovery from major stressor?
        - Multi-method: Self-report + observer ratings + physiological markers
        Confidence: ±0.08

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Repair capacity after conflict
        - High RA (8-10): Conflicts end with reconciliation; relationship strengthens
        - Low RA (0-4): Conflicts leave lasting damage; no repair; resentment builds

    Organizations: Crisis response capacity
        - High RA (8-10): Org rebounds from setbacks; learns from failures
        - Low RA (0-4): Setbacks cause permanent damage; morale never recovers

    Consciousness: Stress resilience, emotional regulation
        - High RA (8-10): Person recovers quickly from stress; maintains equilibrium
        - Low RA (0-4): Stress causes lasting dysregulation; rumination; no recovery

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: 7-10 (robust recovery; anti-fragile)
    Viable systems: 5-7 (adequate recovery)
    Stressed systems: 3-5 (impaired recovery; resources depleted)
    Crisis systems: 0-3 (no recovery capacity; failures cascade)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Determines damping coefficient (δ) in Tstab calculations: High RA = 
      negative damping (oscillations die out). Low RA = positive damping 
      (oscillations amplify).
    - Enables CA_comp (compensatory coordination): Recovery potential provides 
      resources for compensation
    - Inversely correlated with Sthermo (thermodynamic entropy): Exhausted 
      systems cannot recover

             III. CATEGORY 2: ENTROPY CHANNELS (6 CONSTRUCTS)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Entropy measures disorder, uncertainty, and unpredictability in system state. 
Higher entropy indicates the system is harder to predict, more chaotic, and 
closer to collapse. The framework distinguishes six types of entropy operating 
at different scales and domains.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #8: Smicro (MICROCOORDINATION ENTROPY)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Microcoordination Entropy
ABBREVIATION: Smicro
SCALE: 0-10 (continuous, higher = more entropy = worse)
CATEGORY: Entropy Channel

DEFINITION:
Uncertainty in moment-to-moment coordination between system elements. High 
Smicro means you cannot predict whether coordination will succeed or fail in 
any given interaction. This is the entropy of "micro-level defection."

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Game theory: Defection in iterated prisoner's dilemma increases uncertainty
- Relational dynamics: Unpredictable cooperation/defection patterns
- Organizational behavior: Task-level coordination failures

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 5 minutes):
        Method: Defection rate count
        - Ask: "In the last week, how many times did someone fail to follow 
          through on a small commitment or expectation?"
        - Count: Micro-defections (forgotten tasks, broken micro-promises)
        - Score: Min(defections/week, 10)
        Confidence: ±0.3

    Tier 2 (Standard, 6 hours over 2 weeks):
        Method: 2-week daily diary + defection coding
        - System members log all coordination attempts daily
        - Code each: Successful coordination vs. Failed coordination (defection)
        - Calculate: Defection rate = failures / total attempts
        - Score: (defection_rate × 100) capped at 10
        Confidence: ±0.12

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, 12 weeks):
        Method: Longitudinal diary + multi-rater coding + outcome prediction
        - 12-week daily diary from all members
        - Independent raters code defections (ICC ≥ 0.70)
        - Test: Does baseline Smicro predict breakup/crisis at 6-12 months?
        - Experimental manipulation (if ethical): Increase/decrease defections; 
          measure effect on Ω
        Confidence: ±0.08

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Unpredictability of partner follow-through
        - Low Smicro (0-3): Partner reliably does what they say; predictable
        - High Smicro (7-10): Partner randomly forgets commitments; unreliable

    Organizations: Task-level coordination failures
        - Low Smicro (0-3): Team members reliably deliver on micro-commitments
        - High Smicro (7-10): Constant dropped balls; no one knows who will deliver

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: 0-2 (very low micro-defection)
    Viable systems: 2-4 (occasional micro-defections, quickly repaired)
    Stressed systems: 4-7 (frequent micro-defections)
    Crisis systems: 7-10 (chaos; constant unpredictability)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Reduced by high CA_gen and CA_comp: Strong coordination reduces defections

CONSTRUCT #9: Smeso (MESOSCALE ENTROPY)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Mesoscale Entropy
ABBREVIATION: Smeso
SCALE: 0-10 (continuous, higher = more entropy = worse)
CATEGORY: Entropy Channel

DEFINITION:
Uncertainty in the overall state of the relationship or system. High Smeso 
means you cannot predict whether the system will be "together" or "separate," 
"engaged" or "withdrawn," on any given day. This is uncertainty at the level 
of system identity.

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Relational entropy: Uncertainty in relationship state (Sloman)
- Attachment theory: Insecure attachment = unpredictable availability
- Organizational entropy: Ambiguous membership, unclear commitment

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 5 minutes):
        Method: Absenteeism/separation days count
        - Ask: "In the last month, how many days were you unexpectedly apart/
          disengaged when you expected to be together/engaged?"
        - Count: Unexpected separation events (not planned separations)
        - Score: Min(separation_days/month, 10)
        Confidence: ±0.3

    Tier 2 (Standard, 6 hours over 2 weeks):
        Method: Forced vs. voluntary separation log
        - System members log daily: "Today, was our connection voluntary 
          (chosen) or forced (obligatory)?"
        - Calculate: % days where connection felt forced or uncertain
        - Score: (% forced_or_uncertain / 10) × 10
        Confidence: ±0.12

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, 12 weeks):
        Method: Daily state logs + uncertainty calculation
        - 12-week daily diary: Rate relationship state on 1-10 scale each day
        - Calculate Shannon entropy: H = -Σ p(state) log p(state)
          where p(state) = frequency of each state level (1, 2, 3, ... 10)
        - High entropy: State varies unpredictably day-to-day
        - Low entropy: State is consistent and predictable
        - Score: Normalized H (0-10 scale)
        Confidence: ±0.08

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Relationship state uncertainty
        - Low Smeso (0-3): Couple consistently together; predictable connection
        - High Smeso (7-10): "Are we together today?" is genuinely uncertain

    Organizations: Membership/engagement uncertainty
        - Low Smeso (0-3): Employees consistently engaged and present
        - High Smeso (7-10): Constant turnover; unclear who's committed

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: 0-2 (very stable state)
    Viable systems: 2-4 (occasional uncertainty, quickly resolved)
    Stressed systems: 4-7 (frequent uncertainty about system state)
    Crisis systems: 7-10 (complete unpredictability; system barely exists)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Amplified by Smicro: Micro-defections cascade to state uncertainty
    - Reduced by high CI (consent integrity): Genuine commitment reduces uncertainty
    - Inversely correlated with Tstab: High Smeso → low Tstab (unstable)

- Psychopathology: Fragmented self-concept in borderline, dissociative disorders

        - Multi-rater: ICC ≥ 0.75 between independent raters

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:

    - Amplified by high Smicro/Smeso: External unpredictability creates internal chaos

CONSTRUCT #14: HERS (HARMFUL EXIT RESTRICTION SCORE)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Harmful Exit Restriction Score
ABBREVIATION: HERS
SCALE: 0-10 (continuous, higher = more restriction = worse)
CATEGORY: Abuse/Exploitation

DEFINITION:
The degree to which system members are prevented from leaving by external 
barriers (economic, social, physical, emotional). High HERS indicates 
entrapment: members stay not by choice but because leaving is too costly.

THEORETICAL BASIS:

- Organizational psychology: Golden handcuffs and sunk costs

          • Emotional: Low self-esteem, fear, trauma bonding
        - Score: Min(total_barriers, 10)
        Confidence: ±0.3

    Tier 2 (Standard, 2 hours):
        Method: Exit barrier audit (structured interview)
        - Systematic assessment across 4 domains (0-10 each):
          1. Economic barriers: Income dependence, asset entanglement
          2. Social barriers: Network overlap, isolation from alternatives
          3. Physical barriers: Geography, health, custody arrangements
          4. Emotional barriers: Self-esteem, fear, trauma bonds
        - Score: Average of 4 domain scores
        - Note: High HERS + high ADR/EFI = severe abuse pattern
        Confidence: ±0.15

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, legal/financial audit):
        Method: Comprehensive verification
        - Legal review: Custody arrangements, restraining orders, financial liens
        - Financial analysis: Income sources, asset ownership, debt structure
        - Social network analysis: Map support network; identify isolation
        - Clinical assessment: Trauma, self-esteem, fear levels
        - Outcome: Does HERS predict inability to leave abusive situations?
        Confidence: ±0.08

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Partner entrapment
        - Low HERS (0-3): Both partners could leave if they chose; stay voluntarily
        - High HERS (7-10): One or both partners trapped by external factors

    Organizations: Employee entrapment
        - Low HERS (0-3): Employees can leave easily; stay by choice
        - High HERS (7-10): Employees trapped by golden handcuffs, non-competes, 
          visas, etc.

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: 0-2 (minimal exit barriers, genuine freedom)
    Viable systems: 2-4 (some practical barriers, but exit possible)
    Stressed systems: 4-6 (significant barriers; exit difficult)
    Crisis systems: 6-10 (severe entrapment; exit nearly impossible)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Amplifies effect of ADR/EFI: Abuse + entrapment = severe exploitation
    - Overridden by low CI: If CI = 0 (full consent integrity), HERS doesn't 
      matter (genuine freedom to exit)
    - Reduces RA: Trapped systems cannot recover (no exit option = no threat 
      to abuser)

    Viable systems: 2-4 (occasional external attribution, mostly internal focus)

    Crisis systems: 7-10 (identity depends on enemies; internal purges)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Often co-occurs with high ADR/EFI: Scapegoats receive punishment
    - Reduces HA: Scapegoating eliminates diversity (purge of different voices)
    - Indicates low organizational Π: Cannot integrate contradictions; must expel them


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #18: Severity (ABUSE SEVERITY SCORE)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Abuse Severity Score
ABBREVIATION: Severity
SCALE: 0-10 (continuous, higher = more severe = worse)
CATEGORY: Abuse/Exploitation

DEFINITION:
The worst-case severity of abuse incidents, regardless of frequency. High 
Severity indicates life-threatening or permanently damaging abuse. Low Severity 
indicates minor harms or no abuse. This is distinct from ADR (frequency).

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Domestic violence lethality assessment: Severity predicts future danger
- Trauma research: Single severe trauma can cause lasting damage
- Risk assessment: Severity determines intervention urgency

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 5 minutes):
        Method: Type categorization
        - Ask: "What is the worst thing that has happened in this system?"
        - Categorize by severity:
          0-2: Verbal criticism, minor conflict
          3-5: Yelling, property damage, intimidation
          6-8: Physical assault, threats, severe control
          9-10: Life-threatening violence, severe injury, rape
        - Score: Category value
        Confidence: ±0.3

    Tier 2 (Standard, 15 minutes):
        Method: Validated severity scale
        - Administer CTS2 (Conflict Tactics Scale) or PMWI (Psychological 
          Maltreatment of Women Inventory)
        - Score highest severity item endorsed
        - Interview: Contextualize severity in system dynamics
        - Score: Scale value
        Confidence: ±0.12

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, longitudinal tracking):
        Method: Medical/legal record verification + trajectory
        - Review medical records (injuries documented?)
        - Review legal records (police reports, protection orders?)
        - Longitudinal: Is severity escalating over time?
        - Lethality assessment: Use validated tool (DVSI, etc.)
        - Outcome: Does severity predict future violence?
        Confidence: ±0.08

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Worst abuse incident
        - Low Severity (0-3): Minor conflicts, no violence
        - High Severity (8-10): Life-threatening violence, severe injury

    Organizations: Worst misconduct
        - Low Severity (0-3): Minor ethical lapses
        - High Severity (8-10): Sexual assault, major fraud, life-endangering negligence

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: 0 (no abuse)
    Viable systems: 0-2 (no abuse or very minor incidents)
    Stressed systems: 2-5 (moderate incidents emerging)
    Crisis systems: 5-10 (severe abuse; safety risk)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Multiplied with Prevalence: Severity × Prevalence = systemic abuse score
    - Amplified by HERS: High Severity + high HERS = life-threatening situation
    - Triggers emergency interventions: Severity > 7.0 → immediate safety planning


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #19: Prevalence (ABUSE PREVALENCE / BREADTH)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Abuse Prevalence (Breadth of Harm)
ABBREVIATION: Prevalence
SCALE: 0-10 (continuous, higher = more widespread = worse)
CATEGORY: Abuse/Exploitation

DEFINITION:
The proportion of system members affected by abuse. High Prevalence indicates 
abuse is systemic (affects many people). Low Prevalence indicates abuse is 
isolated (one victim) or absent. This is distinct from ADR (frequency) and 
Severity (intensity).

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Organizational misconduct: Prevalence indicates cultural problem vs. bad actor
- Public health: Prevalence measures population-level risk
- Systemic abuse: Widespread abuse indicates structural, not individual, issue

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 5 minutes):
        Method: Person count
        - Ask: "How many people in this system have experienced harm?"
        - Count: # affected individuals
        - Score: (# affected / total system size) × 10
        Confidence: ±0.3

    Tier 2 (Standard, 2 hours):
        Method: Population audit (confidential survey)
        - Anonymous survey to all members: "Have you experienced [abuse types]?"
        - Calculate: % reporting any abuse
        - Score: (% affected / 10) × 10
        - Note: May undercount due to fear of reporting
        Confidence: ±0.15

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, 12 months):
        Method: Longitudinal tracking + multi-source verification
        - Baseline population survey (anonymous)
        - 12-month follow-up: New incidents? New victims?
        - Multi-source: Victim reports, witness reports, records
        - Outcome: Does baseline prevalence predict organizational dysfunction?
        Confidence: ±0.08

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Number of people harmed (typically 1 victim in 2-person dyad)
        - Low Prevalence (0-2): No abuse or mutual low-level conflict
        - High Prevalence (8-10): Both partners harmed (mutual abuse) or one 
          severely victimized

    Organizations: % of employees experiencing abuse
        - Low Prevalence (0-2): <5% affected (isolated incidents)
        - High Prevalence (7-10): >30% affected (systemic culture of abuse)

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: 0 (no abuse)
    Viable systems: 0-2 (<5% affected)
    Stressed systems: 2-5 (10-20% affected)
    Crisis systems: 5-10 (>30% affected; systemic abuse)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Multiplied with Severity: Severity × Prevalence in abuse formula
    - Indicates systemic vs. individual problem: High Prevalence = culture change 
      needed, not just removing one bad actor
    - High Prevalence + high EFI = totalitarian system (fear-based control of masses)

           V. CATEGORY 4: INTEGRITY & COHERENCE (4 CONSTRUCTS)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

These constructs measure fundamental properties of system structure: whether 
membership is voluntary (consent), whether rules are transparent (opacity), 
whether identity is authentic (authorship), and whether individuals are 
internally coherent (Π).

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #20: CI (CONSENT INTEGRITY)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Consent Integrity
ABBREVIATION: CI
SCALE: 0-1 (binary or continuous)
CATEGORY: Integrity / Fundamental Property

DEFINITION:
The degree to which system membership is genuinely voluntary and exit is freely 
possible. CI = 1 (full consent) means members stay by authentic choice. CI = 0 
(no consent) means members are trapped. This is the most fundamental construct: 
without consent, nothing else matters.

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Political philosophy: Consent is foundation of legitimate authority (Locke, Rawls)
- Ethics: Autonomy as prerequisite for moral relationships
- Domestic violence: Exit capacity determines whether relationship is partnership 
  or captivity

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 1 minute):
        Method: Single question
        - Ask: "Can you leave this relationship/organization tomorrow if you 
          wanted to, without severe negative consequences?"
        - Binary: Yes (CI = 1) or No (CI = 0)
        - Continuous alternative: "On 0-10 scale, how free are you to leave?"
          Convert to 0-1 scale (divide by 10)
        Confidence: ±0.2

    Tier 2 (Standard, 30 minutes):
        Method: 3-axis structured interview
        - Assess three dimensions of consent (each 0-1):
          1. Economic freedom: Can you support yourself if you leave?
          2. Social freedom: Will you lose critical relationships if you leave?
          3. Psychological freedom: Do you believe you can leave?
        - Score: Average of 3 dimensions
        - Note: All three must be present for genuine consent
        Confidence: ±0.15

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, comprehensive boundary mapping):
        Method: Multi-domain consent verification
        - Legal: Review contracts, custody arrangements, financial entanglements
        - Economic: Assess independent income, assets, credit access
        - Social: Map support network; identify isolation
        - Psychological: Clinical assessment of agency beliefs, learned helplessness
        - Physical: Assess mobility, health constraints
        - Outcome: Does baseline CI predict ability to exit if system becomes abusive?
        - Validation: Compare self-reported CI to objective exit capacity
        Confidence: ±0.08

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Voluntary partnership vs. entrapment
        - CI = 1 (full consent): Both partners free to leave; stay by choice
        - CI = 0 (no consent): One or both partners trapped; relationship is captivity

    Organizations: Voluntary employment vs. coercion
        - CI = 1 (full consent): Employees can leave without catastrophic consequences
        - CI = 0 (no consent): Employees trapped (visa dependency, non-competes, etc.)

CRITICAL ROLE IN FORMULA:

    Abuse term: Abuse = (ADR + EFI + HERS + Severity/Prevalence) / 5 × (1 - CI)
    
    If CI = 1 (full consent): Abuse term = 0 (even if abuse present, it's 
                               context-dependent and member chooses to stay)
    If CI = 0 (no consent): Abuse term = full weight (exploitation is occurring)

This makes CI a **veto construct**: full consent (CI = 1) removes abuse weight 
entirely. The framework assumes that in genuinely voluntary relationships, 
members have agency and can leave if harm becomes intolerable.

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: CI = 0.9-1.0 (full consent)
    Viable systems: CI = 0.7-0.9 (mostly voluntary, some constraints)
    Stressed systems: CI = 0.4-0.7 (exit difficult but possible)
    Crisis systems: CI = 0.0-0.4 (severe entrapment)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Overrides entire Abuse category: CI = 1 → Abuse weight = 0
    - Inversely correlated with HERS: Low CI usually means high HERS (exit restricted)
    - Fundamental prerequisite: Without consent, system is not a partnership but 
      a prison


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #21: E-Opacity (ENVIRONMENTAL OPACITY)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Environmental Opacity
ABBREVIATION: E-Opacity
SCALE: 0-1 (binary or continuous)
CATEGORY: Opacity / Structural Property

DEFINITION:
The degree to which system rules, expectations, and consequences are hidden, 
ambiguous, or unpredictable. High opacity (E-Opacity = 1) means members cannot 
predict what will happen; they operate in fog. Low opacity (E-Opacity = 0) 
means rules are transparent and consequences predictable.

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Organizational transparency: Clear rules enable coordination
- Legal theory: Rule of law requires knowable rules
- Psychology of control: Unpredictability causes learned helplessness

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 5 minutes):
        Method: Expert judgment or member perception
        - Ask: "On a scale of 0-10, how often are you surprised by what's 
          considered acceptable or unacceptable here?"
        - Score: Rating / 10 (convert to 0-1 scale)
        - Or expert rates opacity based on policy review
        Confidence: ±0.2

    Tier 2 (Standard, 1 hour):
        Method: Rule documentation audit
        - List all explicit rules/norms (written or stated)
        - Interview members: "Which rules are clear? Which are ambiguous?"
        - Calculate: % rules that are (a) documented, (b) communicated, 
          (c) consistently enforced
        - Score: 1 - (% clear_and_consistent)
        Confidence: ±0.15

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, stakeholder interviews + feedback):
        Method: Multi-source transparency assessment
        - Survey all members: Perceived rule clarity (0-10 scale)
        - Review documentation: Are policies written? Accessible?
        - Enforcement audit: Same rule violation → same consequence?
        - Outcome: Does opacity predict coordination failure or abuse?
        Confidence: ±0.08

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Implicit relationship rules
        - E-Opacity = 0: Clear expectations, consistent boundaries
        - E-Opacity = 1: "Walking on eggshells"; unpredictable reactions

    Organizations: Policy transparency
        - E-Opacity = 0: Rules documented, communicated, consistently applied
        - E-Opacity = 1: Secret rules, arbitrary enforcement, political minefield

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: 0.0-0.2 (high transparency)
    Viable systems: 0.2-0.4 (mostly clear, some ambiguity)
    Stressed systems: 0.4-0.7 (increasing opacity, confusion)
    Crisis systems: 0.7-1.0 (near-total opacity, unpredictability)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Amplifies Smacro (macroscale entropy): Opacity is structural uncertainty
    - Enables ADR/EFI: Hidden rules allow arbitrary punishment
    - Reduces IAE effectiveness: Cannot adapt if you can't see the environment


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #22: Coherence-WA (COHERENCE WITHOUT AUTHORSHIP)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Coherence Without Authorship
ABBREVIATION: Coherence-WA
SCALE: 0-1 (binary or continuous)
CATEGORY: Coherence / Authenticity

DEFINITION:
The degree to which system coherence is imposed from outside rather than 
emerging from authentic internal integration. High Coherence-WA (= 1) indicates 
"borrowed coherence" (cult, totalitarian org, coercive relationship). Low 
Coherence-WA (= 0) indicates authentic coherence from within.

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Authoritarian psychology: Externally imposed identity vs. autonomous identity
- Cult dynamics: Charismatic leader provides coherence members cannot generate
- Developmental psychology: True self vs. false self (Winnicott)

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 10 minutes):
        Method: Narrative review
        - Ask: "Tell me who you are and what matters to you"
        - Listen for: Does person reference external authority for all answers?
          ("My leader says I am...", "The organization believes...")
        - Binary: Borrowed coherence (1) vs. Internal coherence (0)
        Confidence: ±0.2

    Tier 2 (Standard, 2 hours):
        Method: Narrative coding + questioning
        - 30-min narrative interview: "Describe your values, identity, goals"
        - Code for: Origin of coherence (internal reflection vs. external prescription)
        - Test: Ask about contradictions. Can person integrate them independently, 
          or do they defer to authority?
        - Score: % responses that reference external authority (convert to 0-1)
        Confidence: ±0.15

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, comprehensive assessment):
        Method: Multi-component authorship test
        - 90-min narrative interview with probing
        - Origin audit: "Where did that belief come from? How did you develop it?"
        - Flexibility test: "What if [authority] changed their view? Would yours change?"
        - Compare to: Validated scales for autonomy, external locus of control
        - Outcome: Does Coherence-WA predict exit difficulty, identity collapse 
          if authority removed?
        Confidence: ±0.08

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Individuals: Autonomous vs. borrowed identity
        - Coherence-WA = 0: Person has integrated identity developed through experience
        - Coherence-WA = 1: Person's coherence depends on external authority; 
          identity would collapse if authority removed

    Organizations: Authentic culture vs. imposed ideology
        - Coherence-WA = 0: Culture emerges from shared values, co-created
        - Coherence-WA = 1: Ideology imposed by charismatic leader; disappears 
          if leader leaves

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: 0.0-0.2 (authentic coherence)
    Viable systems: 0.2-0.4 (mostly authentic, some external influence)
    Stressed systems: 0.4-0.7 (increasing dependence on external authority)
    Crisis systems: 0.7-1.0 (total borrowed coherence; cult-like)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - High Coherence-WA often co-occurs with high ADR/EFI/HERS: Coercive systems 
      impose coherence
    - Related to CI: Low CI often means high Coherence-WA (can't leave because 
      identity depends on staying)
    - Opposite of Π: High Π (individual coherence) usually means low Coherence-WA 
      (internal authorship)


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #23: Π (PI - INDIVIDUAL COHERENCE)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Individual Coherence (Pi)
ABBREVIATION: Π
SCALE: 0-10 (continuous)
CATEGORY: Coherence / Fundamental Property

DEFINITION:
The degree to which an individual (or system) has an integrated, consistent, 
and stable sense of self, values, and narrative. High Π indicates coherent 
identity and meaning-making capacity. Low Π indicates fragmentation, 
contradiction, and confusion. This is the opposite of Spsy (psychological entropy).

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Narrative psychology: Coherent life story predicts well-being
- Psychotherapy: Integration is goal of many therapeutic approaches
- Consciousness research: Integration as core feature of consciousness (Tononi IIT)
- Identity theory: Stable self-concept enables agency

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 1 minute):
        Method: Self-rating
        - Ask: "On a scale of 0-10, how coherent and integrated do you feel 
          as a person? Do your beliefs, values, and actions hang together?"
        - Score: Self-reported rating
        Confidence: ±0.3

    Tier 2 (Standard, 15 minutes):
        Method: Life Engagement Test (LET) or similar validated scale
        - Administer LET (6-item scale measuring meaning and coherence)
        - Brief interview: "Describe your core values. Do your actions align?"
        - Score: Composite of scale + interview
        Confidence: ±0.12

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, 3 hours + longitudinal):
        Method: 90-min narrative interview + 5-axis decomposition
        - Extended life narrative: "Tell your life story"
        - Rate on 5 dimensions (0-10 each):
          1. Narrative consistency: Does story hang together?
          2. Emotional-behavioral alignment: Emotions match actions?
          3. Value-action alignment: Stated values match behavior?
          4. Identity stability: Self-description consistent over time?
          5. Meaning-making capacity: Can integrate contradictions?
        - Score: Average of 5 dimensions
        - Longitudinal: Assess Π at 3 time points; measure stability
        - Multi-rater: ICC ≥ 0.75 between independent raters
        Confidence: ±0.08

v23.6 DECOMPOSITION:
Π can be broken into 5 components for granular assessment:
    1. Narrative Consistency (Π_narrative)
    2. Emotional-Behavioral Alignment (Π_affect)
    3. Value-Action Alignment (Π_ethics)
    4. Identity Stability (Π_identity)
    5. Meaning-Making Capacity (Π_integration)

This allows identification of specific coherence deficits (e.g., high narrative 
consistency but low value-action alignment = hypocrisy).

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Individuals: Personal coherence
        - High Π (8-10): Integrated sense of self, coherent life story
        - Low Π (0-4): Fragmented identity, contradictory beliefs, confusion

    Couples: Individual coherence of partners (average both)
        - Both high Π (7-10): Secure individuals; can maintain separate identities
        - One or both low Π (0-5): Fusion, enmeshment, or fragmentation

    Organizations: Leadership coherence (see ρ for transmission)
        - High leader Π (7-10): Authentic leadership, clear vision
        - Low leader Π (0-5): Inconsistent leadership, contradictory directives

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: Individual Π = 7-10
    Viable systems: Individual Π = 5-7
    Stressed systems: Individual Π = 3-5
    Crisis systems: Individual Π = 0-3

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Inversely correlated with Spsy: Π measures coherence; Spsy measures entropy 
      (opposite constructs measuring same phenomenon)
    - Enhanced by high IAE: Adaptive capacity enables integration
    - Transmits via ρ (leadership coupling): Leader Π affects team outcomes
    - dΠ/dt measures rate of change: Is coherence increasing or decreasing?

          VI. CATEGORY 5: TEMPORAL & LEADERSHIP (3 CONSTRUCTS)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

These three constructs were added in v23.6 to capture dynamic properties: 
temporal stability, rate of coherence change, and leader-to-system coupling.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #24: Tstab (TEMPORAL STABILITY INDEX)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Temporal Stability Index
ABBREVIATION: Tstab
SCALE: 0-1 (continuous, 1 = perfect stability, 0 = maximum chaos)
CATEGORY: Temporal Dynamics

DEFINITION:
A composite measure of how stable system state is over time. Incorporates 
oscillation frequency, amplitude, and damping. High Tstab indicates small, 
damped oscillations (stable). Low Tstab indicates large, amplifying oscillations 
(approaching collapse).

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Dynamical systems: Oscillation dynamics predict stability vs. chaos
- Control theory: Damping coefficient determines system stability
- Relationship dynamics: Volatility predicts breakup (Gottman)

FORMULA:
    Tstab = [f × A × (1 - |δ|)] / normalization_constant
    
    Where:
        f = Oscillation frequency (cycles per month)
        A = Amplitude (average peak-to-valley distance, 0-10 scale)
        δ = Damping coefficient (negative = stabilizing, positive = destabilizing)
    
    Normalized so:
        Most unstable: f=3, A=5, δ=+0.1 → Tstab ≈ 0.15
        Moderately stable: f=1, A=2, δ=-0.05 → Tstab ≈ 0.6
        Very stable: f=0.3, A=1, δ=-0.1 → Tstab ≈ 0.85

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 5 minutes):
        Method: Visual inspection + self-report
        - Show wave diagrams: smooth, choppy, storm
        - Ask: "Which best represents your situation?"
        - Ask: "Are things getting better, worse, or staying the same?"
        - Estimate Tstab from pattern + trend
        Confidence: ±0.2

    Tier 2 (Standard, 4 hours over 30 days):
        Method: 30-day oscillation calculation
        - Collect 30 daily state ratings (1-10 scale)
        - Calculate:
          • Frequency: Count peaks, divide by 30 days
          • Amplitude: Average distance from mean to peaks
          • Damping: Compare early vs. late amplitude (declining = negative δ)
        - Apply Tstab formula
        Confidence: ±0.12

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, 90 days):
        Method: Frequency/amplitude/damping decomposition
        - 90-day daily state tracking
        - Spectral analysis (FFT) to identify dominant frequency
        - Exponential regression to estimate damping: A(t) = A₀ * e^(δt)
        - Test: Does baseline Tstab predict crisis timing?
        Confidence: ±0.08

v23.6 DECISION TREE THRESHOLDS:

    Tstab ≤ 0.2 → EMERGENCY (imminent collapse, 1-4 weeks)
        Action: Override Ω to ≤ 3.0; immediate intervention required
        Probability: 85-95% collapse within window without intervention
    
    Tstab ≤ 0.4 → CRISIS (high-risk window, 1-3 months)
        Action: Apply -1.5 penalty to Ω; intervention within 1-2 weeks
        Probability: 70-80% collapse without intervention
    
    Tstab ≤ 0.7 → MONITOR (caution zone, quarterly review sufficient)
        Action: Standard care; monitor Tstab quarterly
        Probability: 20-30% collapse within 6 months
    
    Tstab > 0.7 → STABLE (low risk)
        Action: Maintenance intervention; annual review
        Probability: <5% collapse within 12 months

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Couples: Relationship volatility and stability
        - Tstab > 0.7: Conflicts are rare, small, and resolve quickly (damping)
        - Tstab 0.4-0.7: Moderate volatility, manageable
        - Tstab < 0.4: High volatility, conflicts escalating (crisis)

    Organizations: Performance stability
        - Tstab > 0.7: Steady performance, predictable operations
        - Tstab < 0.4: Wild swings, chaos, approaching failure

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: Tstab = 0.8-1.0
    Viable systems: Tstab = 0.6-0.8
    Stressed systems: Tstab = 0.4-0.6
    Crisis systems: Tstab = 0.0-0.4

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Influenced by RA (recovery potential): High RA → negative δ → higher Tstab
    - Influenced by Stemporal: High Stemporal → low Tstab
    - Modifies dX/d weight: Low Tstab + positive trajectory = unstable improvement 
      (doesn't count as much)
    - Can override Ω: Very low Tstab (< 0.2) forces emergency classification


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #25: dΠ/dt (COHERENCE VELOCITY)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Coherence Velocity (Rate of Change in Individual Coherence)
ABBREVIATION: dΠ/dt
SCALE: -2 to +2 per year (continuous, negative = declining, positive = improving)
CATEGORY: Temporal Dynamics

DEFINITION:
The rate at which individual (or system) coherence is changing over time. 
Positive dΠ/dt indicates increasing integration, healing, or development. 
Negative dΠ/dt indicates fragmentation, deterioration, or disintegration.

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Psychotherapy research: Rate of early improvement predicts final outcome
- Developmental psychology: Coherence increases with maturation (normally)
- Trauma research: Trauma causes coherence collapse (negative dΠ/dt)

FORMULA:
    dΠ/dt = (Π_final - Π_initial) / time_interval
    
    Typically measured quarterly or monthly:
        dΠ/dt_monthly = (Π_month3 - Π_month1) / 2 months
        dΠ/dt_yearly = (Π_year1 - Π_baseline) / 1 year

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 5 minutes):
        Method: Month-to-month change estimate
        - Ask: "Compared to a month ago, do you feel more or less integrated 
          as a person?"
        - Rating scale: -10 (much worse) to +10 (much better)
        - Convert to yearly rate: (rating / 10) × 12 months
        Confidence: ±0.4

    Tier 2 (Standard, 3 hours over 3 months):
        Method: Quarterly Π tracking
        - Measure Π at baseline, 1 month, 2 months, 3 months (4 time points)
        - Calculate slope: Linear regression of Π over time
        - Extract: dΠ/dt = slope per month × 12 (convert to yearly rate)
        Confidence: ±0.2

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, 12 months):
        Method: Multi-point longitudinal assessment
        - Measure Π quarterly (5 time points over 12 months)
        - Fit growth curve model (linear, quadratic, or exponential)
        - Extract velocity parameter
        - Test: Does early dΠ/dt (months 0-3) predict final Π (month 12)?
        Confidence: ±0.1

v23.6 THRESHOLDS:

    dΠ/dt < -0.5/year → RAPID DECLINE (apply -1.5 penalty to Ω)
        Interpretation: Coherence collapsing; crisis intervention needed
    
    -0.5 ≤ dΠ/dt < 0 → GRADUAL DECLINE (monitor closely)
        Interpretation: Slow deterioration; intervention before acceleration
    
    dΠ/dt ≈ 0 → STABLE (no change)
        Interpretation: Stagnation (could be good if Π already high)
    
    0 < dΠ/dt ≤ +0.5/year → GRADUAL IMPROVEMENT (positive sign)
        Interpretation: Healing, development, therapy working
    
    dΠ/dt > +0.5/year → RAPID IMPROVEMENT (very positive)
        Interpretation: Transformative change, accelerated growth

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Therapy: Client progress indicator
        - Positive dΠ/dt: Therapy working, coherence increasing
        - Negative dΠ/dt: Therapy not working or client deteriorating

    Leadership: Leader development trajectory
        - Positive dΠ/dt: Leader maturing, becoming more consistent
        - Negative dΠ/dt: Leader under stress, coherence declining

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: dΠ/dt = +0.3 to +1.0 (sustained growth)
    Viable systems: dΠ/dt = -0.2 to +0.3 (stable or slight improvement)
    Stressed systems: dΠ/dt = -0.5 to -0.2 (declining)
    Crisis systems: dΠ/dt = -2.0 to -0.5 (rapid collapse)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Rate of change in Π (individual coherence)
    - Parallel to dX/d (trajectory): dX/d measures system state; dΠ/dt measures 
      coherence state
    - Early dΠ/dt in therapy predicts outcome: Rapid early gain → good final outcome


─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CONSTRUCT #26: ρ (RHO - LEADERSHIP COUPLING COEFFICIENT)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FULL NAME: Leadership Coupling Coefficient (Rho)
ABBREVIATION: ρ
SCALE: 0-1 (continuous, 0 = no coupling, 1 = perfect coupling)
CATEGORY: Leadership / Contagion

DEFINITION:
The degree to which a leader's internal state (coherence, stress, emotion) 
transmits to the team or organization. High ρ indicates strong coupling: when 
leader is stressed, team becomes stressed. Low ρ indicates independence: team 
state is buffered from leader state.

THEORETICAL BASIS:
- Emotional contagion: Leader emotions spread to followers (Barsade)
- Systems dynamics: Coupling coefficient in dynamical systems
- Organizational behavior: Leader mood affects team performance

FORMULA:
    ρ = Correlation(Leader_Π, Team_Outcome)
    
    Or more precisely:
        ρ = ∂(Team_State) / ∂(Leader_Π)
    
    Measured empirically by tracking leader state and team state simultaneously 
    and calculating correlation or regression coefficient.

MEASUREMENT PROTOCOLS:

    Tier 1 (Rapid, 10 minutes):
        Method: Observation + team member report
        - Observe leader-team interaction (single meeting)
        - Ask team members: "When your leader is stressed, how much does it 
          affect you?"
        - Rating: 0 (no effect) to 10 (completely affected)
        - Score: Average rating / 10 (convert to 0-1)
        Confidence: ±0.2

    Tier 2 (Standard, 8 hours over 6 months):
        Method: 6-month correlation tracking
        - Monthly assessment of:
          • Leader Π (coherence)
          • Leader stress level (0-10)
          • Team engagement (average across team members)
          • Team performance metric
        - Calculate: Correlation(Leader_state, Team_state)
        - Score: Absolute value of correlation (r)
        Confidence: ±0.15

    Tier 3 (Research-grade, 12 months):
        Method: Network + regression analysis
        - 12-month daily tracking:
          • Leader daily state (Π, stress, mood)
          • Each team member's daily state
        - Network analysis: Map influence pathways
        - Regression: Predict team state from leader state (controlling for 
          other factors)
        - Extract: β coefficient = ρ
        - Test: Does high ρ + low leader Π predict poor team outcomes?
        Confidence: ±0.08

v23.6 THRESHOLDS:

    ρ < 0.3 → LOW COUPLING (team buffered from leader)
        Interpretation: Team resilient; doesn't depend on leader state
        Risk: May also mean leader has little influence (good or bad)
    
    0.3 ≤ ρ < 0.6 → MODERATE COUPLING (typical)
        Interpretation: Leader influences team but doesn't dominate
    
    ρ ≥ 0.6 → HIGH COUPLING (team dependent on leader)
        Interpretation: Leader state strongly affects team
        Risk: If leader Π low, team suffers
        Formula adjustment: If Π_leader < 5.0 AND ρ > 0.6:
            Apply penalty: -0.1 × (5.0 - Π_leader) × ρ

DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INTERPRETATION:

    Organizations: Leader-to-team transmission
        - Low ρ (0.0-0.3): Team autonomous, resilient to leader mood
        - High ρ (0.7-1.0): Team "catches" leader's state (good or bad)

    Families: Parent-to-child transmission
        - Low ρ: Children buffered from parent stress
        - High ρ: Parent stress/coherence directly affects children

TYPICAL SCORES BY VIABILITY STATE:
    Thriving systems: ρ = 0.3-0.5 (moderate coupling, high leader Π)
    Viable systems: ρ = 0.4-0.7 (varies; acceptable if leader Π high)
    Stressed systems: ρ = 0.6-0.9 (high coupling, leader Π declining)
    Crisis systems: ρ = 0.7-1.0 (extreme dependence, low leader Π = team collapse)

INTERACTIONS WITH OTHER CONSTRUCTS:
    - Amplifies effect of leader Π: High ρ × low leader Π = team dysfunction
    - Can be protective if leader Π high: High ρ × high leader Π = team thriving
    - Related to HA (heterogeneity): Low HA (homogeneous team) often has high ρ 
      (groupthink, no buffering)
    - Interventions: Reduce ρ (build team autonomy) OR increase leader Π (leader development)

     VII. HOW THE CONSTRUCTS WORK TOGETHER: SYSTEMIC INTEGRATION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

THE OMEGA FORMULA REVISITED:

    Ω = [Positive / (Entropy × 1.0 + Abuse × 0.5)] - ConcentrationPenalty ± TemporalAdjustments

This formula represents a **balance of forces**:

NUMERATOR: Positive Factors (IAE, CA, HA, HE, dX/d, RA)
    These are the system's adaptive capacities, resources, and growth potential.
    Higher positive factors → system can handle more stress and complexity

DENOMINATOR: Entropy + Abuse
    Entropy (Smicro, Smeso, Smacro, Sthermo, Spsy, Stemporal): Disorder, 
        unpredictability, chaos. Weighted 1.0× (full impact).
    Abuse (ADR, EFI, HERS, Severity, Prevalence): Exploitation, coercion, harm. 
        Weighted 0.5× (significant but moderated).
        Multiplied by (1 - CI): If consent is perfect (CI = 1), abuse weight = 0.

    Higher entropy/abuse → system viability drops rapidly

CONCENTRATION PENALTY:
    If any single construct > 8.0, subtract 1.0 from Ω.
    Rationale: Over-reliance on one factor creates brittleness (single point of failure)

TEMPORAL ADJUSTMENTS:
    Three dynamic modifiers:
    1. If dΠ/dt < -0.5/year: -1.5 penalty (rapid coherence collapse)
    2. If Tstab < 0.3: -1.0 × (0.3-Tstab)/0.3 (instability penalty)
    3. If Π_leader < 5.0 AND ρ > 0.6: -0.1 × (5.0-Π_leader) × ρ (leader contagion)

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
KEY INTERACTION PATTERNS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. THE ENTROPY CASCADE:
   Smicro (micro-defections) → Smeso (state uncertainty) → Smacro (normative collapse)
   
   Small coordination failures accumulate into systemic chaos. High Smicro today 
   predicts high Smeso next month predicts high Smacro next year.

2. THE CONSENT OVERRIDE:
   CI = 1 (full consent) → Abuse term = 0
   
   If system membership is genuinely voluntary, abuse factors are neutralized. 
   The framework assumes agency: if you can leave but choose to stay, you have 
   reasons the formula doesn't capture (e.g., consensual BDSM, high-intensity jobs).

3. THE COHERENCE-ENTROPY DUALITY:
   Π (individual coherence) ≈ 10 - Spsy (psychological entropy)
   
   Coherence and entropy measure the same phenomenon from opposite directions. 
   Systems with high Π have low Spsy and vice versa. Both constructs are included 
   for measurement flexibility (Π via self-report, Spsy via linguistic analysis).

4. THE RECOVERY AMPLIFIER:
   RA (recovery potential) determines damping coefficient (δ) in Tstab
   
   Systems with high RA show negative damping (oscillations die out) → high Tstab.
   Systems with low RA show positive damping (oscillations amplify) → low Tstab.
   RA is thus the most important positive factor for long-term stability.

5. THE HETEROGENEITY PARADOX:
   High HA (diversity) increases adaptive capacity BUT requires high IAE to coordinate
   
   Diverse teams are more innovative (high CA_gen) but also risk fragmentation 
   (high Smeso) if they lack information-processing capacity (low IAE). The 
   optimal strategy: maximize HA AND IAE simultaneously.

6. THE TEMPORAL STABILITY GATE:
   Tstab < 0.4 → Apply crisis interventions regardless of Ω score
   
   Even if overall Ω appears viable (e.g., 5.0), very low Tstab indicates 
   imminent collapse. The decision tree overrides Ω in these cases: temporal 
   dynamics take precedence over static assessment.

7. THE LEADERSHIP AMPLIFIER:
   High ρ (coupling) × Low leader Π = Team collapse
   High ρ (coupling) × High leader Π = Team thriving
   
   Leader coherence transmits to team. Interventions: (a) increase leader Π 
   (coaching, support) or (b) decrease ρ (build team autonomy, diversify leadership).

8. THE ABUSE-ENTROPY INTERACTION:
   High Abuse → High Entropy (bidirectional causation)
   
   Abuse increases unpredictability (Smeso, Smacro): "Walking on eggshells" = 
   high entropy. High entropy enables abuse: Chaos creates opportunities for 
   exploitation. Systems must address both simultaneously.

9. THE TRAJECTORY-STABILITY INTERACTION:
   Positive dX/d (improving) + Low Tstab (unstable) = Unsustainable improvement
   
   If system is improving rapidly but unstably, the gains won't last. Formula 
   can adjust dX/d weight by Tstab factor: Weight_dX/d × Tstab. Stable 
   improvement counts more than volatile improvement.

10. THE ENVIRONMENTAL ENRICHMENT BUFFER:
    High HE (enrichment) reduces impact of high Sthermo (stress)
    
    Novel experiences and engagement buffer against burnout. Systems under high 
    stress (Sthermo) can maintain viability if HE remains high (e.g., challenging 
    but meaningful work vs. repetitive stressful work).
